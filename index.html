Research Question: How do billing costs vary among hospitals in relation to the insurance provider?

Healthcare has become a highly-contested, highly-politicized topic in the United States due to the notoriously high healthcare costs. Private equity relationships with hospitals, rising inflation, and government policies have only exacerbated this problem. There are many factors in the final medical bill issued including the specifics of hospital, condition, and insurance provider. In this project, we investigate how billing costs for identical medical conditions influence these variations. Using this dataset, we will compare and analyze charges across institutions to assess its potential impact on healthcare accessibility while considering insurance type and identifying the patterns and potential drivers of the cost variation. Similar studies reveal the magnitude of this issue with over 70% of Medicare and uninsured admits being classified as emergencies (Burke et al., 2017).     Conclusion of Findings
  • The initial static visualization demonstrates how age group may be correlated with the billing costs per insurance type. From this, it is reasonable to conclude that there is not a significant deviation in costs according to age except for the youngest age group (0-17 years old), where blue cross costs are relatively higher and aetna costs are significantly lower.

  • From the second static visualization, we can see the rise and fall of billing costs for treatment of a single condition each year between 2019 and 2024. These changing rates could potentially reflect changes in the economy, which should especially be taken into account for 2020-2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic. During these years, we can see the billing costs noticeably increase for each medical condition. This can be seen with diabetes, asthma, and hypertension.

  • The interactive visualizatin demonstrates how each hospital charges for treatment of the same condition based on the insurance provider. It is clear that not every hospital accepts the majority of insurance types, which is seen in Wilson PLC Hospital and INC Jackson Hospital. Medical care for the same condition for the same providers can vary by up to $30,0000 according to the bar chart. Although it is important to consider the length of each hospital stay, these singificant disparities show how individual hospitals may have varying negotiated rates with insurance companies.

  • Overall, all three of the visualizations highlight major problems with affordable access to care. Medicare and Aetna appear to have the highest billing costs obseved across the visualizations. With Medicare in mind, this suggests that elderly citizens may experience higher hospital billing costs. By continuing to shed light on this issue, we can hope that a change comes to protect the healthcare access of all people in the United States, especially the elderly.

  • Future considerations for this kind of data investigatin may include using real-world data to compare to this synthetic data. It may be important to consider this research question using organic data since synthetic data oftentimes is not reproducible using real-world data. Although synthetic data can sometimes help depcict accurate statistical correlations and distributions, it will not be able to capture outliers that occur in real data as well as more complex relationships.

  • It would also be worthwhile to depict any geospatial relationships among this data, which could potentially reflect the economy across different parts of the country or even the world.
